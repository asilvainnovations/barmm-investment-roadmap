Causal Loop Diagrams (CLDs) map cause-and-effect relationships. Reinforcing loops (R) amplify change. Balancing loops (B) seek equilibrium. These diagrams reveal the non-linear system dynamics that must be actively managed to achieve transformative change.

Reinforcing Loops — Virtuous Cycles

These amplifying loops create self-sustaining momentum when activated.

R1: Investment–Development Virtuous Cycle

When investments flow into priority sectors (halal, agro-industry, tourism), they generate employment and income growth, expanding the domestic consumer market, improving business viability, and attracting additional investors. This virtuous cycle requires sustained catalyst investments to reach self-sustaining velocity.

Leverage Points:
  • L7 (Positive Feedback Gain): Accelerate virtuous cycles through investment incentives tied to employment generation.
  • L9 (Delay Lengths): Reduce time between investment approval and project implementation through streamlined permitting.
  • L10 (Stock-Flow Structure): Build critical mass of anchor investments to trigger self-sustaining momentum.

R2: Governance–Investor Confidence Loop

Improved governance (transparency, rule of law, investor protection) increases investor confidence, which drives investment, generates economic growth, expands the tax base, and provides resources for further governance improvements. The first elected Bangsamoro Parliament represents a critical juncture in this loop.

Leverage Points:
  • L3 (Goals): Tie political legitimacy to investment climate improvements.
  • L5 (Rules): Establish investor protection laws with enforcement mechanisms.
  • L6 (Information Flows): Publish investment climate indicators and governance performance metrics.

Balancing Loops — Constraints & Stabilizers

These loops represent resistance or limits that must be proactively managed.

B1: Security–Investment Tensions

Security incidents—even isolated rido or armed group activity—trigger investor caution, reducing investment flows and slowing development that would otherwise contribute to stability. This loop highlights the critical importance of sustaining and communicating BARMM's security improvements to break negative narrative cycles.

Leverage Points:
  • L6 (Information Flows): Publish real-time security incident data and peace dividend metrics.
  • L8 (Negative Feedback): Strengthen community-based conflict early warning and response systems.
  • L5 (Rules): Establish rapid response mechanisms for investor protection during security incidents.

B2: Growth–Resource Constraints (Limits to Growth)

Rapid investment growth encounters balancing pressures from infrastructure bottlenecks (unreliable energy, poor roads), skills shortages (59.3% literacy), and environmental limits. Without proactive capacity investment, growth plateaus as the system hits resource ceilings.

Leverage Points:
  • L10 (Stock-Flow Structure): Complete ZBIP, deploy farm-to-market roads, expand broadband to 60% by 2030.
  • L9 (Delay Lengths): Reduce business registration from multiple days to 1 day via BEGMP.
  • L11 (Buffer Sizes): Build 20% reserve capacity in power generation and food security stocks.

Systems archetypes are recurring structural patterns that reveal root causes of behavior and guide leverage point design. The BIRD framework identifies five critical archetypes constraining BARMM's investment ecosystem.

1. Limits to Growth

Rapid investment growth encounters balancing pressures from infrastructure bottlenecks, skills shortages, and environmental limits. Without proactive capacity investment, growth plateaus.

Intervention: Front-load ZBIP, farm-to-market roads, and literacy/TVET alignment to remove binding constraints before momentum stalls.

2. Fixes that Fail

Ad-hoc tax incentives without systemic governance reform provide short-term investor relief but generate unintended consequences — revenue loss and weak institutions. The "solution" becomes the disease.

Intervention: Pair all investment incentives with mandatory institutional capacity milestones (BHB accreditation, digital registration, aftercare scaling). Address root causes through systemic governance reforms.

3. Success to the Successful

Initial advantages in mainland provinces (Maguindanao del Norte, Lanao del Sur) compound, attracting more resources while archipelagic areas (Tawi-Tawi, Sulu, Basilan) face capital starvation, deepening regional inequality.

Intervention: Implement affirmative investment policies, provincial equity targets, and satellite BBOI offices in Sulu, Tawi-Tawi, Basilan. Establish sectoral balance criteria.

4. Tragedy of the Commons

Fragmented resource governance across the Foundations cluster depletes watersheds, forests, and grid capacity as individual actors maximize short-term yields from shared resources.

Intervention: Enact Forestry Code, operationalize JMC No. 2026-01, and institutionalize co-management frameworks for watersheds and marine protected areas.

5. Growth & Underinvestment

Investment approvals surge (₱5.1B in Q1 2026) but facilitation capacity (BBOI, BHB, aftercare) lags behind, creating bottlenecks that degrade performance and reduce perceived ROI for further capacity investment.

Intervention: Expand institutional capacity ahead of demand: hire/train 100+ halal certifiers, 50+ Islamic finance professionals. Build 20% slack in facilitation systems.
