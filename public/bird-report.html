Abstract

The Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao (BARMM) stands at a critical juncture in its development trajectory. This paper presents the Bangsamoro Investment Roadmap 2026–2035 (BIRD), a comprehensive, evidence-based strategic framework designed to transform BARMM into Southeast Asia's premier hub for resilient, ethical, and inclusive investment. Drawing on the Bangsamoro Economic and Investment Ecosystem (BEIE) Framework and the Strat Planner Pro Architecture, the roadmap integrates systems thinking, robust quantitative diagnostics, and multi-criteria strategic evaluation to identify high-leverage investment opportunities across five functional clusters: Foundations, Transformers, Enablers, Connectors, and Financiers.

Through quantified SWOT analysis, systems archetype mapping, and causal loop diagramming, this study diagnoses binding constraints and identifies transformative leverage points across BARMM's political, economic, social, environmental, and technological domains. Four strategic options were evaluated using a weighted seven-criteria decision matrix: Halal Economy Dominance Strategy (HEDS), Green Economy Monetization Strategy (GEMS), Infrastructure-First Enabling Strategy (IFES), and Integrated Ecosystem Development Strategy (IEDS). The IEDS emerged as the optimal pathway with a composite score of 8.33/10, synchronizing cross-cluster investments to achieve a projected GRDP of PHP 550 billion by 2035 while reducing poverty incidence to below 20%.

The roadmap is anchored on six guiding principles—Moral Governance, Ecosystem Thinking, Cultural Authenticity, Environmental Stewardship, Inclusivity, and Data-Driven Decision Making—and operationalized through a Balanced Scorecard with 30+ key performance indicators, three implementation phases (2026–2035), and a comprehensive monitoring and evaluation framework aligned with OIC/SMIIC international standards, the Philippine Development Plan 2023–2028, and the UN Sustainable Development Goals.

Keywords: Bangsamoro Investment Roadmap; BEIE Framework; halal economy; Islamic finance; systems thinking; strategic planning; BIMP-EAGA; sustainable development; Moral Governance; BARMM; OIC/SMIIC standards

Chapter 1

Introduction: Setting the Stage

1.1 Background and Context

The Bangsamoro Autonomous Region in Muslim Mindanao (BARMM) stands at a pivotal moment in its development trajectory. Established through the Bangsamoro Organic Law (Republic Act No. 11054, 2018), the region inherited decades of conflict, underdevelopment, and institutional fragility. Yet, within these challenges lie extraordinary opportunities: a young and growing population, abundant natural resources, a strategic geographic position within the Brunei-Indonesia-Malaysia-Philippines East ASEAN Growth Area (BIMP-EAGA), and a rich cultural heritage rooted in Islamic values that provide unique competitive advantages in the global halal economy and Islamic finance sectors (Silva & Unsi, 2026).

The BARMM economy has demonstrated remarkable resilience, posting 6.2% GRDP growth in 2025 and maintaining positive trajectories across key indicators (PSA-BARMM, 2025). Investment approvals reached PHP 5.1 billion in the first four months of 2026 alone, signaling growing investor confidence (BOI-MTIT, 2026). However, poverty incidence remains elevated at 30.1%, infrastructure deficits persist across all provinces, and the region's absorptive capacity for large-scale investments remains constrained by governance fragmentation and human capital gaps (MFBM, 2025).

The global economic landscape presents both headwinds and tailwinds for BARMM. The International Monetary Fund (IMF) projects global GDP growth at 2.9% in 2026, down from 3.2% in 2025, with rising geopolitical fragmentation reshaping trade patterns (World Bank, 2025). Meanwhile, the global halal market is projected to exceed USD 3.2 trillion by 2026, Islamic finance assets are growing at double-digit rates, and climate adaptation financing is expanding rapidly (IsDB, 2025). These dynamics create a narrow but significant window of opportunity for BARMM to position itself as a preferred destination for ethical, sustainable, and Shariah-compliant investments.

1.2 Scope and Limitations

The temporal scope of this roadmap spans 2026–2035, organized into three implementation phases aligned with BARMM's governance milestones and sectoral investment readiness: Phase 1 (Foundation Building, 2026–2028) focuses on establishing governance infrastructure, halal certification systems, and enabling conditions; Phase 2 (Acceleration, 2029–2032) scales investment pipelines, expands value chains, and activates green economy revenue streams; and Phase 3 (Consolidation and Global Integration, 2033–2035) positions BARMM as a recognized halal, agro-industrial, and sustainable investment destination (BOI-MTIT, 2026).

The geographic scope encompasses all five BARMM provinces—Basilan, Lanao del Sur, Maguindanao del Norte, Maguindanao del Sur, and Sulu—plus the cities of Cotabato and Lamitan, and the special geographic area of 63 barangays in Cotabato Province. The sectoral scope covers all five BEIE functional clusters: Foundations (agriculture, fisheries, forestry, energy), Transformers (industry, manufacturing, logistics, halal processing), Enablers (infrastructure, health, education, human capital), Connectors (tourism, digital connectivity, trade, BIMP-EAGA integration), and Financiers (Islamic banking, takaful, microfinance).

1.3 Purpose of the Investment Roadmap

The Bangsamoro Investment Roadmap Development (BIRD) 2026–2035 serves five specific purposes. First, it provides a comprehensive, research-based, and policy-oriented investment framework that integrates provincial economic insights, global investment trends, workshop outputs, and multi-sector strategic analysis. Second, it guides policymakers, planners, and investors over the next decade through evidence-based strategies grounded in quantitative diagnostics. Third, it applies integrated analytical frameworks—the BEIE Framework and Strat Planner Pro—to identify high-leverage investment opportunities. Fourth, it establishes measurable targets and monitoring mechanisms aligned with international standards including OIC/SMIIC halal certification frameworks, Environmental, Social, and Governance (ESG) criteria, the Philippine Development Plan 2023–2028, and Bangsamoro-specific objectives. Fifth, it fosters synchronized ecosystem development rather than scattered, siloed interventions (BOI-MTIT, 2026).

1.4 Guiding Principles

The BIRD 2026–2035 is grounded in six guiding principles that shape all strategic recommendations and implementation approaches (Silva & Unsi, 2026). These principles are not merely aspirational; they are operationalized through specific mechanisms, indicators, and accountability frameworks embedded throughout the roadmap.

1.6 Analytical Frameworks and Methodology

The BIRD 2026–2035 employs two complementary analytical frameworks: the Bangsamoro Economic and Investment Ecosystem (BEIE) Framework and the Strat Planner Pro Architecture. The BEIE Framework reframes investment analysis away from isolated sectoral silos toward an integrated view of how economic clusters interact and reinforce each other, grounded in systems-based economic theory (Silva, 2026).

The Strat Planner Pro Architecture provides the strategic planning and investment roadmapping engine, integrating five analytical tools: (1) SWOT Analysis with robust mathematical formulations; (2) Systems Thinking and Systems Mapping including causal loop diagrams and archetype identification; (3) Strategic Options and the Investment Roadmap using TOWS matrix and multi-criteria evaluation; (4) Metrics and KPI Benchmarking aligned with OIC/SMIIC standards; and (5) Balanced Scorecard for strategy translation into actionable objectives, measures, targets, and initiatives across four perspectives—Financial, Stakeholder, Internal Process, and Learning & Growth (Silva, 2026).

The methodology follows a diagnostic-generative-evaluative-prescriptive sequence: (1) diagnose system constraints and leverage points using quantified SWOT and systems archetypes; (2) generate logically matched strategies via TOWS and BEIE cluster linkages; (3) cluster into coherent strategic options with distinct value logics and risk profiles; (4) evaluate using a weighted, evidence-based scoring matrix; and (5) recommend the highest-scoring option with explicit feasibility mitigation and implementation architecture. This ensures the recommended strategy is not aspirational but analytically rigorous, systemically aligned, financially grounded, and institutionally actionable.

Chapter 2

Deep Presencing: Context Analysis Using the BEIE Framework

2.1 Global Economic Trends: Implications for BARMM

This chapter applies the BEIE Framework as a living systems lens, moving beyond traditional environmental scanning toward an integrated, relational understanding of how political, economic, social, environmental, and technological (PEDSET) factors interact within and across BARMM's five functional clusters. The PEDSET domains are not treated as isolated categories but are distributed across the BEIE clusters where they functionally operate (Silva, 2026).

Global economic dynamics from 2025–2026 are reshaping investment prospects in BARMM. Slowing global growth, shifting trade patterns, and rising energy and food prices create both risks and opportunities across the region's BEIE clusters. While the Bangsamoro economy has demonstrated resilience—posting 2.7% GRDP growth in 2024 amid global headwinds—it remains vulnerable to external shocks, particularly in agriculture, energy, and fiscal stability (PSA-BARMM, 2025).

The global halal market presents a particularly significant opportunity. With the Muslim population projected to reach 2.2 billion globally and halal food, finance, cosmetics, and pharmaceuticals markets expanding at compound annual growth rates of 6–10%, BARMM's cultural legitimacy as a Muslim-majority autonomous region provides a natural competitive advantage that no other Philippine region can credibly replicate (Thomson Reuters & DinarStandard, 2021). The Islamic Development Bank projects Islamic finance assets to exceed USD 4 trillion by 2026, creating substantial opportunity for BARMM's Financiers Cluster (IsDB, 2025).

2.2 Cross-Cutting Operating Systems

The BEIE Framework positions Moral Governance, Resilience, Inclusivity, and Peace not as aspirational goals but as the foundational operating systems that determine whether all five functional clusters function effectively, equitably, and sustainably. Workshop consultations (Workshops 1–5, 2026) consistently identified three systemic bottlenecks that constrain investment momentum: (1) fragmented program delivery across Bangsamoro Ministries, Offices, and Agencies (BMOAs); (2) weak inter-agency coordination that produces duplication and gaps; and (3) narrative-driven investor hesitation rooted in perceptions of governance instability (Silva & Unsi, 2026).

Moral Governance serves as the ethical compass of the Bangsamoro development agenda, promoting accountability, transparency, integrity, and service-oriented leadership. Key actors include the Bangsamoro Parliament, Office of the Chief Minister, MILG-BARMM, Civil Society Organizations, and Development Partners. Moral Governance creates investor confidence by reducing uncertainty and strengthening institutional credibility.

Resilience ensures that communities, institutions, and businesses can withstand shocks ranging from climate-related disasters to economic disruptions and social conflicts. Building resilience requires adaptive infrastructure, disaster preparedness systems, climate-smart agriculture, and diversified economic opportunities. Inclusivity ensures that economic growth benefits all sectors of society, particularly women, youth, indigenous peoples, fisherfolk, farmers, and vulnerable communities. Peace remains one of BARMM's most valuable development assets, providing the stability necessary for long-term investment planning and economic expansion (BPRO, 2025).

2.3 BEIE Cluster Deep Dives

The Foundations Cluster (Cluster 1) consists of agriculture, fisheries, forestry, energy, and environment—the resource base of raw materials providing sustainable inputs to all other clusters. Key ministries include MAFAR and MENRE, with the Southern Philippines Development Authority (SPDA) serving as a critical implementing agency. BARMM's agriculture sector, while employing the majority of the population, faces productivity gaps linked to fragmented value chains and limited access to modern inputs. The fisheries sector holds significant export potential but requires cold-chain infrastructure and international certification. The energy sub-sector is transitioning toward renewable sources under BSEMP targets of 75.86% renewable energy by 2028 (MENRE-BARMM, 2024).

The Transformers Cluster (Cluster 2) encompasses industry, manufacturing, logistics, and halal processing—the value-adding transformation of Foundation resources. The halal industry holds particular strategic significance. BARMM possesses a natural comparative advantage in halal production because of its Muslim-majority population, Islamic institutions, and cultural legitimacy. The Bangsamoro Halal Industry Development Plan 2025–2030 outlines a comprehensive strategy for ecosystem development, certification infrastructure, and export market linkages, positioning BARMM to capture a share of the USD 2.3 trillion global halal market (MTIT-BARMM, 2025).

The Enablers Cluster (Cluster 3) provides the physical, social, and institutional infrastructure necessary for economic growth: infrastructure, social services, health, education, and human capital. Infrastructure reduces transaction costs, improves accessibility, and enhances competitiveness. Human capital is arguably BARMM's most important long-term investment asset, with the education budget reaching PHP 32.65 billion in 2026—the largest single allocation in the regional budget (BPDA, 2023).

The Connectors Cluster (Cluster 4) creates access to markets through tourism, digital connectivity, trade, exports, and BIMP-EAGA integration. BARMM's strategic location within BIMP-EAGA creates opportunities for cross-border trade, regional logistics, halal market access, maritime connectivity, tourism corridors, and deeper ASEAN integration. The Financiers Cluster (Cluster 5) provides financial capital access aligned with Shariah principles through Islamic banking, takaful (cooperative insurance), and microfinance, expanding financial inclusion while strengthening trust within Muslim communities.

2.4 Provincial Economic Outlooks

BARMM's provinces exhibit diverse economic profiles, resource endowments, and investment readiness levels. Basilan, recently declared ASG-free, shows strong agricultural and fisheries potential. Lanao del Sur, with Marawi City as its capital, offers significant Islamic finance and cultural tourism opportunities. Maguindanao del Norte and del Sur, the newest provinces, require substantial infrastructure investment but possess rich agricultural land and energy resources. Sulu and Tawi-Tawi, archipelagic provinces, face unique connectivity challenges but hold strategic maritime and fisheries potential.

Strategic Diagnostics: SWOT and Systems Mapping

3.1 Quantified SWOT Analysis

The Strat Planner Pro framework employs robust mathematical formulations for each SWOT quadrant to ensure quantitative rigor in strategic assessment. This approach moves beyond qualitative SWOT checklists toward data-driven diagnostics that can be tracked, compared, and updated over time (Silva, 2026).

SWOT Mathematical Formulations

StrengthsRI = (Impact × Likelihood) / 51–5Higher values = stronger resilience capacity
OpportunitiesRI = √(Impact × Likelihood)1–5Higher values = greater resilience through opportunity exploitation
WeaknessesRisk = Impact × Likelihood1–25Higher values = higher internal risk exposure
ThreatsVI = (Impact² × Likelihood) / 251–5Higher values = greater vulnerability to external threats

The quantified SWOT analysis reveals that BARMM's highest-strength assets include halal legitimacy (RI = 5.0), youthful demographics (RI = 5.0), and BIMP-EAGA location (RI = 4.0). Conversely, the most critical weaknesses include infrastructure deficits (Risk = 25.0) and human capital gaps (Risk = 20.0), which represent binding constraints that must be addressed for any investment strategy to succeed.

3.2 Systems Archetypes and Causal Loop Diagrams

Systems thinking provides a way of understanding reality that emphasizes the relationships among a system's parts rather than simply the parts themselves. Following Senge (1990), systems thinking holds that the ability to see the whole of a phenomenon in its broader context will provide new and different insights than can be gained by looking at each of its component parts individually. For BARMM, traditional investment lists treat sectors as standalone silos—neat, organized, and ultimately misleading (Silva, 2026).

Four key systems archetypes were identified through causal loop diagramming and stakeholder consultation: (1) Limits to Growth—rapid investment growth encounters balancing pressures from infrastructure bottlenecks, skills shortages, and environmental limits; (2) Fixes that Fail—short-term relief from incentives delays systemic reform, leading to institutional weakness persistence; (3) Tragedy of the Commons—individual actors maximize short-term yields from shared resources but collectively deplete the ecological base; and (4) Success to the Successful—early investment winners attract disproportionate resources, creating provincial disparities (Meadows, 1999; Ricigliano, 2011).

3.3 Leverage Points Analysis

Not all interventions are equal. Changing a parameter (a number) has little leverage. Changing information flows has more. Changing system goals or paradigms can transform everything. Applying Meadows' (1999) hierarchy of leverage points, the analysis identified four critical transformative interventions for BARMM: (1) L3 Goals—redefining success metrics to include sustainability, inclusivity, and cross-cluster integration; (2) L5 Rules—changing incentive structures to reward long-term thinking and penalize siloed behavior; (3) L6 Information Flows—making performance gaps transparent to all stakeholders through the MEL Dashboard; and (4) L10 Stock-Flow Structure—rearranging physical and organizational capacity to enable cluster-to-cluster resource flows.

Chapter 4

Strategic Options and the Seven-Criteria Evaluation Matrix

4.1 Strategy Formulation Methodology

The TOWS (Threats-Opportunities-Weaknesses-Strengths) matrix serves as the analytical bridge between diagnostic findings and strategic recommendations (Weihrich, 1982). Rather than treating SWOT as a static inventory, the Strat Planner Pro framework derives actionable strategies by cross-referencing internal factors with external conditions. Four strategic categories emerge: SO strategies (maxi-maxi) leverage strengths to exploit opportunities; WO strategies (mini-maxi) overcome weaknesses by capitalizing on opportunities; ST strategies (maxi-mini) use strengths to mitigate threats; and WT strategies (mini-mini) minimize both weaknesses and threats through defensive positioning (Silva, 2026).

For BARMM, the TOWS analysis generated 24 candidate strategies distributed across the four quadrants. However, not all strategies are equally viable or impactful. A rigorous prioritization framework was therefore developed to evaluate each strategic option against seven weighted criteria that reflect BARMM's unique developmental context (Silva, 2026).

4.2 The Seven-Criteria Evaluation Framework

The 7-Criteria Evaluation Matrix was designed specifically for post-conflict, transitioning economies where standard investment criteria must be augmented with governance, peace, and resilience considerations. Each criterion is scored on a 1–10 scale, with weights reflecting institutional stakeholder priorities derived from the 16-section validation survey (see Chapter 8).

Chapter 5

The Integrated Ecosystem Development Strategy (IEDS)

5.1 Strategy Architecture and Pillar Integration

The Integrated Ecosystem Development Strategy (IEDS) represents a paradigmatic departure from conventional sectoral investment planning. Rather than treating halal industry, governance reform, infrastructure development, and Islamic finance as competing priorities requiring trade-offs, IEDS positions them as mutually reinforcing ecosystem components operating within the broader BEIE framework (Silva, 2026). The strategy is built on the premise that BARMM's economic transformation requires simultaneous progress across all four pillars, with each pillar serving as both input and output to the others.

The architectural design of IEDS draws on ecosystem theory, which posits that sustainable economic systems function not as collections of isolated entities but as networks of interdependent actors, resources, and flows (Moore, 1993). Within the BEIE framework, Moral Governance serves as the ecosystem's keystone species, the central organizing principle that ensures all economic activity aligns with ethical, inclusive, and sustainable values (Silva, 2026). This is not merely a governance overlay; it is a fundamental design parameter that shapes incentive structures, investment criteria, and performance metrics.

5.2 The Four Strategic Pillars

5.2.1 Pillar 1: Halal Industry and Ecosystem Development

The halal industry pillar aims to position BARMM as the Philippines' premier halal hub and a recognized player in the global halal economy, projected to reach USD 3.1 trillion by 2028 (DinarStandard, 2023). BARMM's competitive advantage in this sector is rooted in its demographic composition (over 90% Muslim population), cultural legitimacy, and geographic proximity to ASEAN halal markets. The pillar encompasses three integrated tracks: (1) Halal Certification and Standards, achieving OIC/SMIIC accreditation to ensure international recognition of BARMM halal products; (2) MSME Capacity Building, providing technical assistance, financing access, and market linkages for halal-producing micro, small, and medium enterprises; and (3) Halal Tourism Development, promoting BARMM's Islamic heritage sites, halal-friendly hospitality services, and religious tourism circuits (Silva, 2026).

5.2.2 Pillar 2: Governance and Institutional Reform

Governance reform constitutes the foundational pillar upon which all other investments depend. Drawing on the institutional economics tradition (North, 1990), this pillar recognizes that sustainable economic growth requires credible institutional frameworks that reduce transaction costs, protect property rights, and ensure policy predictability. The governance pillar operates through three mechanisms: (1) Digital Governance (BEGMP), implementing the Bangsamoro Electronic Governance Master Plan to digitize government services, procurement, and citizen engagement; (2) Business Climate Reform, streamlining business registration, permitting, and tax administration to improve the ease of doing business ranking; and (3) Anti-Corruption and Transparency, establishing the Bangsamoro Integrity and Coordination Council (BICC) with public procurement monitoring and asset disclosure systems (Silva, 2026).

5.2.3 Pillar 3: Infrastructure and Connectivity

Infrastructure deficits represent the most binding constraint on BARMM's economic growth, as confirmed by the SWOT analysis (Risk = 25.0). The infrastructure pillar addresses this constraint through a comprehensive connectivity program spanning energy, transport, digital, and water infrastructure. Key initiatives include: (1) Zamboanga Basilan Integrated Power (ZBIP), developing 50MW solar mini-grid installations across underserved municipalities; (2) Digital Connectivity, deploying broadband fiber backbone to achieve 80% barangay-level internet coverage by 2030; (3) Halal Park Development, establishing dedicated industrial zones with cold chain, warehousing, and shared processing facilities for halal MSMEs; and (4) Inter-Island Transport, upgrading roll-on/roll-off (RORO) ferry connections between Basilan, Sulu, Tawi-Tawi, and mainland Mindanao (Silva, 2026).

5.2.4 Pillar 4: Islamic Finance and Inclusive Growth

The Islamic finance pillar addresses the chronic underinvestment in Shariah-compliant financial instruments that exclude a significant portion of BARMM's population from formal financial services. Despite a Muslim-majority population, Islamic banking penetration in the Philippines remains below 1%, compared to 25% in Malaysia and 20% in Indonesia (Islamic Financial Services Board, 2022). This pillar includes: (1) Al-Amanah Islamic Investment Bank Expansion, recapitalizing and expanding the only government-owned Islamic bank to serve as BARMM's development finance institution; (2) Takaful (Islamic Insurance) Framework, establishing cooperative insurance schemes for agriculture, livestock, and microenterprise risks; (3) Waqf Revival, modernizing the management of Islamic endowments to fund education, healthcare, and social infrastructure; and (4) Green Sukuk Issuance, developing Shariah-compliant bonds to finance climate adaptation and renewable energy projects (Silva, 2026).

5.3 Three-Phase Implementation Roadmap

The IEDS is implemented through three sequential phases, each with distinct objectives, resource requirements, and success metrics. The phasing reflects the reality that institutional transformation requires sequential rather than simultaneous attention to different reform areas (Pritchett et al., 2013).

Chapter 6

Investment Framework and Priority Action Programs

6.1 Balanced Scorecard Framework

The Balanced Scorecard (BSC) translates the IEDS strategic vision into measurable performance indicators across four perspectives: Financial, Stakeholder, Internal Process, and Learning & Growth (Kaplan & Norton, 1992). Unlike conventional scorecards that treat perspectives as independent domains, the BARMM BSC incorporates causal linkages between perspectives, recognizing that capacity building (Learning & Growth) enables process improvement (Internal Process), which drives stakeholder satisfaction (Stakeholder), ultimately producing financial returns (Financial) (Silva, 2026).

Chapter 7

Institutional Architecture and Governance

7.1 The Bangsamoro Integrity and Coordination Council (BICC)

Effective implementation of the IEDS requires institutional arrangements that transcend traditional ministerial boundaries. The Bangsamoro Integrity and Coordination Council (BICC) is proposed as the apex oversight body for the IEDS, with a mandate spanning strategic coordination, investment monitoring, anti-corruption oversight, and performance accountability (Silva, 2026). The BICC model draws on successful experiences from Malaysia's Economic Planning Unit and Indonesia's National Development Planning Agency (Bappenas), while being calibrated to BARMM's unique parliamentary system and fiscal architecture.

The BICC operates at three governance tiers: (1) the Ministerial Council, comprising the Chief Minister and relevant ministry heads, which provides policy direction and resolves inter-ministerial disputes; (2) the Technical Working Group, composed of director-level officials and technical specialists, which manages day-to-day coordination and data compilation; and (3) the Citizens' Oversight Panel, comprising civil society, private sector, and academic representatives, which provides independent validation of reported results and ensures transparency (Silva, 2026). This three-tier structure balances bureaucratic efficiency with democratic accountability, addressing the governance trust deficit identified in the systems archetype analysis.

7.3 Fiscal Architecture and Resource Mobilization

BARMM's fiscal architecture is defined by the asymmetric relationship with the national government, primarily through the Annual Block Grant (ABG) mandated by the Bangsamoro Organic Law. The ABG is calculated as 5% of the national internal revenue collections from the region, net of certain deductions, and is projected to average PHP 80–90 billion annually over the plan period (Bangsamoro Government, 2024). While substantial in absolute terms, this allocation is insufficient to fund both recurrent operations and the capital investments required by the IEDS.

The resource mobilization strategy therefore pursues three complementary tracks: (1) Efficiency Gains, reducing leakages through the BICC anti-corruption framework and digitizing procurement processes, with a target of recovering 8–12% of the ABG through efficiency improvements; (2) National Government Co-Financing, accessing national government program budgets (e.g., DPWH infrastructure, DA agriculture, DOE energy) for BARMM-eligible projects, with a target mobilization of PHP 45 billion over 10 years; and (3) Development Partner Financing, engaging multilateral institutions (World Bank, ADB, IsDB, JICA) and bilateral partners for concessional financing and technical assistance, with a target of USD 1.2 billion over 10 years (Silva, 2026).

7.4 Intergovernmental Coordination

The IEDS requires active coordination with the national government, neighboring regions, and BIMP-EAGA member states. The Intergovernmental Coordination Mechanism (ICM) establishes structured dialogue channels at three levels: (1) National-BARMM, through the Intergovernmental Relations Body and existing ministerial-level coordination committees; (2) Regional, through the Mindanao Development Authority (MinDA) and Regional Development Council coordination; and (3) International, through BIMP-EAGA working groups and dedicated BARMM trade offices in Kuala Lumpur, Jakarta, and Jeddah (Silva, 2026).

A critical aspect of intergovernmental coordination is the alignment of the IEDS with the Philippine Development Plan (PDP) 2023–2028 and its successor. The IEDS is designed to be fully compatible with PDP priorities, particularly in areas of infrastructure connectivity, agricultural modernization, and digital transformation, while adding the unique halal industry and Islamic finance dimensions that are specific to BARMM's development trajectory (Silva, 2026).

Chapter 8

Monitoring, Evaluation, and Adaptive Management

8.1 The MEL Dashboard Architecture

The Monitoring, Evaluation, and Learning (MEL) Dashboard serves as the real-time nervous system of the IEDS, aggregating performance data across all four strategic pillars and thirteen KPIs. Drawing on best practices from the World Bank's Results-Based Management framework and the Open Government Partnership's monitoring standards, the MEL Dashboard provides three user views: Command View for ministers and senior officials, featuring high-level traffic-light indicators and trend analytics; Executive View for program managers, with detailed drill-down capabilities into PAP-level progress and expenditure tracking; and Public View, an open-data portal that enables citizens, civil society, and investors to track IEDS implementation progress (Silva, 2026).

The technical architecture of the MEL Dashboard follows a modern cloud-native design, with Supabase as the backend database providing real-time data synchronization, React-based frontend components for interactive visualization, and automated data pipelines that ingest structured reports from line ministries. The dashboard integrates with the BIRD platform's SurveyWizard module to capture citizen perception data and stakeholder feedback as qualitative complements to quantitative KPI tracking (Silva, 2026).

While all thirteen KPIs are tracked, the MEL Dashboard prioritizes six "Pareto KPIs" that collectively explain approximately 80% of the IEDS's expected impact. 8.3 Validation Survey and Stakeholder Feedback

The 16-section BIRD Validation Survey constitutes the primary mechanism for stakeholder input into IEDS implementation. Administered annually through web, mobile, and in-person channels, the survey captures quantitative ratings and qualitative feedback from five respondent segments: government officials, private sector representatives, academic and research institutions, civil society organizations, and development partners (Silva, 2026).

The survey architecture follows a respondent-type branching logic, with each segment receiving a customized set of sections relevant to their expertise and engagement level. Government respondents complete sections on institutional capacity, policy coherence, and inter-ministerial coordination. Private sector respondents focus on investment climate, market access, and regulatory burden. Academic respondents provide technical assessments of sector strategies. Civil society respondents evaluate inclusivity, transparency, and community impact. Development partner respondents assess aid effectiveness and alignment with international best practices (Silva, 2026).

Key survey innovations include interactive question types designed to maximize engagement and data quality: pin-on-image questions for geographic prioritization, word cloud questions for open-ended theme extraction, 2x2 matrix questions for strategic positioning, and 100-point allocation questions for priority ranking. These interactive formats yield richer data than conventional Likert scales while maintaining respondent engagement through the 20-minute average completion time (Silva, 2026).

8.4 Adaptive Management and Strategy Revision

The IEDS is designed as a living strategy rather than a static blueprint. The adaptive management protocol establishes three mechanisms for strategy evolution: (1) Annual Review, conducted each January, assesses KPI progress, validates survey results, and approves minor adjustments to PAP implementation schedules and budgets; (2) Mid-Term Review, scheduled for 2030, conducts a comprehensive evaluation of strategy effectiveness, including an independent impact assessment, and may recommend major strategic pivots or reallocation between pillars; and (3) End-Term Evaluation, conducted in 2035, produces a full accounting of IEDS results and lessons learned to inform the next planning cycle (Silva, 2026).

The adaptive management framework incorporates explicit triggers for strategy revision. These include: significant changes in the political or security environment (e.g., major ceasefire violations, national policy shifts); macroeconomic shocks (e.g., global recession, commodity price spikes); natural disasters or climate emergencies requiring reallocation of resources; technological disruptions creating new opportunities or rendering existing approaches obsolete; and feedback from the validation survey indicating systematic stakeholder dissatisfaction with specific PAPs or pillars (Silva, 2026).

This adaptive approach reflects the complexity and uncertainty inherent in long-term strategic planning for transitioning regions. As noted by Andrews et al. (2017), successful development strategies in fragile contexts are those that combine clear strategic direction with the flexibility to adjust tactics in response to changing circumstances. The IEDS monitoring and evaluation architecture is designed precisely to enable this adaptive capability while maintaining accountability for results.

8.5 Risk Management and Contingency Planning

The IEDS identifies four categories of implementation risk, each with associated mitigation strategies and contingency plans. Political and Security Risks include changes in the peace process, electoral transitions, and localized security incidents. Mitigation involves maintaining strong civilian-military coordination, investing in conflict-sensitive programming, and building cross-party consensus around the IEDS through parliamentary committee engagement. Fiscal Risks include delays in block grant disbursement, lower-than-projected national revenue collections, and currency volatility affecting development partner commitments. Mitigation involves diversifying revenue sources, maintaining a 15% contingency reserve, and front-loading disbursement requests (Silva, 2026).

Implementation Capacity Risks reflect the limited human resource base within BARMM government agencies, particularly in technical fields such as project management, procurement, and monitoring and evaluation. Mitigation includes targeted capacity building through technical assistance embeds, secondments from development partners, and competitive recruitment of qualified BARMM professionals from the diaspora. External Economic Risks include global halal market fluctuations, changes in international trade regimes, and climate-related disruptions to agriculture and fisheries. Mitigation involves developing climate-resilient infrastructure, diversifying export markets across multiple ASEAN and Middle Eastern destinations, and establishing strategic commodity reserves for critical inputs (Silva, 2026).
